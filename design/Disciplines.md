# Disciplines

A discipline is a named field of study that emerges from the Writer's thesis. It is always defined by a **pair of domains**. Since ideas always touch multiple domains, a single-domain thesis is structurally impossible — the available objects and ideas guarantee cross-domain contact.

A thesis that draws from three or more domains surfaces multiple disciplines (one per pair). The **dominant discipline** — the pair with the greatest combined weight in the written work — determines the committee chair and the primary frame of evaluation. Other strong disciplines create special committee dynamics.

There are **21 disciplines** — one for every possible pairing of the seven domains.

---

## How Disciplines Are Named

Each discipline has two registers:

1. **Official name** — what the institution calls the field. Used by the committee, spoken aloud in the defence. Period-appropriate academic terminology.
2. **Private name** — what the Writer calls it internally. Never spoken. Appears once, at the ending, as the Writer's own recognition of what their work became. Literary, personal, sometimes strange.

The committee members don't all use the official name the same way. Each member **spins** the discipline through their own institutional lens — the cleric sees theological implications, the professor sees methodological ones, the bureaucrat sees administrative ones. These spins are not separate names but attitudes expressed through dialogue.

## The 21 Disciplines

### Rule + Faith — Political Theology

**Official name**: Political Theology
**Private name**: The Doctrine of Obedient Bells

The study of how spiritual authority and temporal power sustain each other. Why the church blessed the revolution, and why the state still needs the church to bless.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Takes ownership. "This is fundamentally a question of divine order expressed through governance." Protective, proprietary. |
| Professor | Suspicious. "Political theology is apologetics wearing an academic hat." Questions rigour. |
| Bureaucrat | Interested. "The practical relationship between church and state is worth formalising." Sees utility. |

---

### Rule + Truth — Jurisprudence

**Official name**: Jurisprudence
**Private name**: The Architecture of Approved Conclusions

The philosophy of law: what makes a law legitimate, what makes a judgment true, and whether those are the same question. In this republic, where censorship committees define the bounds of inquiry, jurisprudence becomes the study of how authority decides what can be known.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Wary. "Law without moral foundation is just administered force." Wants ethical grounding. |
| Professor | Engaged. "A proper legal philosophy demands rigorous epistemology." Sees intellectual merit. |
| Bureaucrat | Proprietary. "This is our domain — the machinery of the state." Sees the thesis as commentary on their own institution. |

---

### Rule + Class — Political Economy

**Official name**: Political Economy
**Private name**: The Arithmetic of Who Keeps What

How power organises wealth, and how wealth sustains power. The revolution nationalised everything on paper. Political economy asks what happened in practice — who holds the keys, who signs the receipts, who is warm and who is cold.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Uncomfortable. "Economic questions have moral dimensions your analysis neglects." Wants charity mentioned. |
| Professor | Intellectually engaged but cautious. "Political economy must remain empirical, not polemical." Warns against ideology. |
| Bureaucrat | Alert. "The administration of national resources is a matter of public record, not academic speculation." Defensive. |

---

### Rule + Art — Civil Rhetoric

**Official name**: Civil Rhetoric
**Private name**: The Grammar of Permitted Beauty

How the state shapes expression — not by silencing it, but by defining which expressions are useful. The censorship committee doesn't ban art; it approves art. Civil rhetoric studies the line between beauty that serves and beauty that threatens, and how power draws that line without admitting it exists.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Dismissive. "Rhetoric is a tool. The question is what truth it serves." Wants substance over form. |
| Professor | Interested but nervous. "The study of rhetoric must not itself become rhetorical." Fears the thesis is too clever. |
| Bureaucrat | Attentive. "Expression within responsible bounds is a matter of policy." Sees the thesis as potentially useful or potentially dangerous. |

---

### Rule + Nature — Administrative Natural Philosophy

**Official name**: Administrative Natural Philosophy
**Private name**: The Republic of Measurable Things

Where governance meets the natural world: classification, resource management, the organisation of knowledge about physical reality. The republic inherited the Enlightenment's promise that nature could be catalogued and controlled. This discipline asks whether the catalogue serves understanding or authority.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Indifferent. "The natural world is God's creation; its administration is your concern." Disengaged unless theology surfaces. |
| Professor | Excited. "Finally, a thesis with empirical grounding." Sees rigorous potential. |
| Bureaucrat | Very interested. "The systematic management of natural resources is essential to the war effort." Sees immediate practical value. |

---

### Rule + Morality — Moral Governance

**Official name**: Moral Governance
**Private name**: The Shape of Duty When Nobody Believes

The ethics of power. Whether the state has moral obligations or merely practical ones. Whether "duty" means anything when the people saying it don't believe it anymore. In a republic built on revolutionary ideals that have hollowed out, moral governance is the study of the gap between what the state says and what it does.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Competitive. "Moral authority belongs to the church, not the state." Territorial. |
| Professor | Philosophical. "The relationship between political obligation and moral duty is a classical problem." Sees intellectual tradition. |
| Bureaucrat | Defensive. "The state's moral position is expressed through its laws and institutions." Doesn't want it examined further. |

---

### Faith + Truth — Scholastic Philosophy

**Official name**: Scholastic Philosophy
**Private name**: The Argument God Makes When He Isn't Listening

The old question: can faith and reason coexist? The church says scripture and scholarship are complementary. The academy says knowledge is produced by method, not revelation. Scholastic philosophy sits in the gap, trying to reconcile systems of knowing that may be fundamentally incompatible.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Proprietary. "Scholasticism is the church's intellectual tradition. We welcome its revival." Takes credit. |
| Professor | Grudgingly respectful. "The scholastic method has historical value, but we've moved beyond it." Condescending. |
| Bureaucrat | Bored. "As long as the conclusions don't contradict approved frameworks." Checks the clock. |

---

### Faith + Class — Ecclesiology

**Official name**: Ecclesiology
**Private name**: The Wealth of the Meek

The church as institution: its property, its hierarchy, its survival strategy. Ecclesiology asks how a spiritual body maintains temporal power — and whether it can do so without becoming the thing it's supposed to transcend. The church kept its buildings through the revolution. Ecclesiology asks what that cost.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Extremely defensive. "The church's institutional arrangements are a matter of pastoral care, not political analysis." This is personal. |
| Professor | Detached. "An institutional analysis of the church is a valid academic exercise." Enjoys the cleric's discomfort. |
| Bureaucrat | Watchful. "Church property is technically national property under the Act." Sees administrative implications. |

---

### Faith + Art — Sacred Aesthetics

**Official name**: Sacred Aesthetics
**Private name**: The Colour of a Hymn No One Believes

The intersection of beauty and belief: sacred music, liturgical art, the architecture of worship. Whether the hymn is still beautiful when the congregation no longer believes the words. Whether beauty itself is a form of faith — or its last refuge.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Moved. "Art is how the divine speaks to those who cannot hear scripture." Genuinely engaged. This is the cleric at their best. |
| Professor | Skeptical. "Aesthetics requires critical distance. Devotional art doesn't produce scholarship — it produces devotion." Questions objectivity. |
| Bureaucrat | Puzzled. "What is the practical application of this research?" Doesn't see the point. |

---

### Faith + Nature — Natural Theology

**Official name**: Natural Theology
**Private name**: The God Buried in the Specimen Jar

Reading God's design in the natural world. The claim that creation reveals the creator — that the order of nature proves the existence of a divine architect. In a republic that has separated church and academy, natural theology is a disciplinary orphan: too scientific for the cleric, too theological for the professor.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Hopeful. "If God's hand is visible in nature, then science and faith are reconciled." Wants this to work. |
| Professor | Hostile. "Natural theology is teleological reasoning dressed as empiricism. It assumes its conclusion." Sees bad method. |
| Bureaucrat | Confused. "Does this fall under the church's purview or the academy's?" Genuinely unsure who should chair. |

---

### Faith + Morality — Moral Theology

**Official name**: Moral Theology
**Private name**: The Conscience That Belongs to Everyone Else

Ethics grounded in the divine: conscience as God-given, duty as sacred obligation, sin as the architecture of moral failure. Moral theology asks whether right and wrong need God to exist — and what happens to a society that says yes but has stopped believing.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | This is home ground. "Morality without faith is convention. Convention changes. Only the divine provides fixed moral reference." Authoritative, possibly rigid. |
| Professor | Respectful but firm. "Moral philosophy has developed beyond its theological roots. We must evaluate the argument on its own terms." Wants secular rigour. |
| Bureaucrat | Pragmatic. "Moral theology is useful insofar as it supports civic virtue." Instrumentalises everything. |

---

### Truth + Class — Sociology of Knowledge

**Official name**: Sociology of Knowledge
**Private name**: The Catalogue of Who Gets to Think

Who produces knowledge, who validates it, and who it serves. Whether the university's truth is universal or institutional — whether "approved frameworks" protect scholarship or protect the people who run the scholarship. In a republic that nationalised the academy, the sociology of knowledge is the study of who owns ideas.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Alarmed. "Reducing truth to social position is relativism. If truth depends on who speaks it, then nothing is sacred." Sees heresy. |
| Professor | Threatened. "The academy produces knowledge through method, not through class position. This thesis undermines the institution from within." Very defensive. |
| Bureaucrat | Interested. "Understanding how knowledge is produced and distributed has clear policy applications." Sees administrative value. |

### Truth + Art — Rhetoric and Letters

**Official name**: Rhetoric and Letters
**Private name**: The Truth That Only Metaphors Can Carry

Whether art can say what argument cannot — whether the poem reaches truths that the thesis cannot prove. Rhetoric and Letters studies the relationship between beauty and knowledge, between how something is said and what it means. In a republic that separates the approved from the unapproved, this discipline asks whether form is itself a kind of knowing.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Suspicious. "If truth can be hidden in art, it can also be hidden from the censors." Sees danger. |
| Professor | Divided. "Literary analysis is a valid field, but the claim that art produces knowledge is epistemologically dubious." Wants boundaries. |
| Bureaucrat | Wary. "The relationship between expression and truth is precisely what the censorship committees were established to manage." Sees institutional territory. |

### Truth + Nature — Natural Philosophy

**Official name**: Natural Philosophy
**Private name**: The Weight of Everything Visible

Empirical investigation of the physical world. Observation, measurement, classification, theory. The republic's most respectable intellectual tradition — inherited from the Enlightenment, sheltered by the academy, supervised by the state. Natural philosophy is permitted, even encouraged, as long as its conclusions stay within approved bounds.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Cautious. "The natural world is God's creation. Natural philosophy must not forget its place in the divine order." Keeps theology on the table. |
| Professor | Enthusiastic. "This is proper scholarship. Empirical, methodical, rigorous." Most comfortable here. |
| Bureaucrat | Supportive. "The state values practical knowledge. Natural philosophy that serves the republic's needs is welcome." Wants applications. |

---

### Truth + Morality — Moral Philosophy

**Official name**: Moral Philosophy
**Private name**: The Argument You Have With Yourself at Four in the Morning

Ethics derived from reason rather than revelation. What can be known about right and wrong through thought alone. The oldest of philosophical traditions — and in this republic, the most dangerous, because moral philosophy that doesn't start from approved premises might reach unapproved conclusions.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Respectful but insistent. "Moral philosophy without divine foundation is a building without ground. Elegant, perhaps. Stable, no." Wants theological roots. |
| Professor | At home. "This is the tradition we inherited. Rigorous ethical reasoning within established frameworks." Sees the discipline as the academy's core. |
| Bureaucrat | Nervous. "Moral philosophy that questions the basis of civic duty has... implications." Sees political risk. |

### Class + Art — Cultural Economy

**Official name**: Cultural Economy
**Private name**: The Price of Something Beautiful

Who pays for art, who controls it, who benefits from it. Whether the concert hall is a temple of beauty or a mechanism of class reproduction. Cultural economy examines how artistic expression is shaped by the institutions that fund, display, and censor it — and who those institutions serve.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Marginal. "The church has always patronised the arts. This analysis seems to forget that." Wants credit for sacred art. |
| Professor | Analytical. "Cultural production as an economic and institutional phenomenon — there's real scholarship here." Sees a valid framework. |
| Bureaucrat | Alert. "The allocation of cultural resources is a matter of state policy." Sees the thesis as commentary on arts funding. |

---

### Class + Nature — Agricultural Philosophy

**Official name**: Agricultural Philosophy
**Private name**: The Soil That Doesn't Know It Was Nationalised

The relationship between land, labour, and natural resources. Who owns the earth, who works it, and what the natural world owes (or doesn't owe) to human systems of property. The revolution nationalised the estates. Agricultural philosophy asks what "nationalised" means when the same families still manage the land.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Peripheral. "The land is God's, given to humanity in stewardship." Offers theology that no one asked for. |
| Professor | Engaged. "There's a real tradition here — physiocracy, natural economy. The theoretical foundations are sound." Sees intellectual precedent. |
| Bureaucrat | Very alert. "Agricultural policy is a matter of national security, particularly during wartime." Sees strategic importance and potential sensitivity. |

### Class + Morality — Social Philosophy

**Official name**: Social Philosophy
**Private name**: The Ethics of Being Cold While They Are Warm

Justice as a structural question: whether institutions can be moral or immoral, whether poverty is a policy choice, whether the revolution's promise of equality creates an obligation or just a slogan. Social philosophy sits where ethics meets economics and asks who owes what to whom.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Sympathetic but channelling. "The church has always concerned itself with the poor. Charity is a Christian duty." Redirects toward faith. |
| Professor | Cautious. "Social philosophy must remain analytical, not polemical. The line between scholarship and activism is thin here." Warns against passion. |
| Bureaucrat | Defensive. "The republic provides for its citizens within available resources. Wartime requires sacrifice from everyone." Takes it personally. |

---

### Art + Nature — Aesthetic Naturalism

**Official name**: Aesthetic Naturalism
**Private name**: The Painting the Moth Doesn't Know It's Sitting In

Beauty in the natural world: whether nature is beautiful by design or by accident, whether art that depicts nature reveals truth or imposes order. The specimen jar and the sketch occupy the same desk. Aesthetic naturalism asks whether they're doing the same thing.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Cautiously interested. "If nature's beauty points to God, then aesthetic naturalism is a branch of natural theology." Wants to claim it. |
| Professor | Measured. "Empirical observation and aesthetic judgment must remain distinct. Confusing the two weakens both." Insists on disciplinary boundaries. |
| Bureaucrat | Indifferent. "A thesis on beauty in nature seems... unlikely to cause difficulty." Doesn't see the stakes. Possibly the safest discipline to defend. |

---

### Art + Morality — Ethical Aesthetics

**Official name**: Ethical Aesthetics
**Private name**: The Poem That Knows What You Should Have Done

Whether beauty has moral content. Whether the beautiful and the good are the same thing, or whether art can be morally instructive without being propaganda. In a republic that censors expression, ethical aesthetics asks the central question: is art that makes people feel something dangerous because of what it says, or because feeling itself is uncontrolled?

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Engaged. "Art has always served moral education. The question is whether it serves divine morality or merely human sentiment." Wants theological ethics. |
| Professor | Interested. "The relationship between aesthetic and moral judgment is a genuine philosophical problem." Sees real intellectual work. |
| Bureaucrat | Watchful. "Art with moral content is precisely what the censorship committees were designed to evaluate." Sees the thesis as meta-commentary on their own function. |

---

### Nature + Morality — Natural Ethics

**Official name**: Natural Ethics
**Private name**: The Conscience That Doesn't Need Permission

Whether moral knowledge is natural — whether the body, the instincts, the physical world can teach us right and wrong without scripture, without institutions, without the committee's approval. Natural ethics is the most dangerous discipline in this republic, because it implies that moral authority doesn't belong to anyone. It just is.

| Member | Spin |
|--------|------|
| Cleric | Hostile. "Natural ethics is atheism in a lab coat. If moral knowledge is natural, then revelation is unnecessary — and so is the church." Sees existential threat. |
| Professor | Fascinated but cautious. "The empirical basis of moral intuition is a genuine research question, but the conclusions must be handled carefully." Sees career risk. |
| Bureaucrat | Alarmed. "If conscience is natural and doesn't require institutional validation, then what is the committee for?" Sees the thesis as an attack on their function. |

## Committee Dynamics

The committee always contains the same three members — cleric, professor, bureaucrat — but their roles and relationships shift based on the thesis's discipline(s).

### Chair Assignment

The dominant discipline determines the chair:

| Dominant domain in the pair | Chair |
|-----------------------------|-------|
| Faith | Cleric |
| Truth, Nature, Art | Professor |
| Rule, Class | Bureaucrat |
| Morality | Contested (see below) |

When the dominant discipline contains **Morality**, the chair is determined by the other domain in the pair. When **both** domains would assign the same chair, that member chairs uncontested. When the two domains point to different chairs, the one whose domain has greater weight in the thesis takes the chair.

### Standard Dynamics

**Single dominant discipline** — the normal case. One member chairs, the other two evaluate from their institutional perspective. The spins described above govern their attitudes.

### Special Dynamics

These scenarios trigger when the thesis surfaces multiple strong disciplines — not just a dominant pair, but secondary pairs with significant weight.

#### Co-Chairs

**Trigger**: Two disciplines are nearly equal in strength, and they assign different chairs.

Two members share authority. Neither fully controls the defence. They may agree on questions but disagree on standards. The Writer must satisfy both — which may mean satisfying neither.

*Example*: A thesis drawing equally from Faith+Truth (Scholastic Philosophy, chaired by Cleric) and Truth+Nature (Natural Philosophy, chaired by Professor). The cleric and professor co-chair. The bureaucrat becomes peripheral — possibly relieved, possibly resentful.

#### The Resentful Rival

**Trigger**: A strong secondary discipline would assign a different chair than the dominant discipline does.

One member chairs. Another member believes their domain is underrepresented — that the thesis is really about *their* field, and the chair is wrong. They don't oppose the thesis; they oppose the chair's framing of it. Questions become proxy wars. The Writer must navigate a committee that is arguing with itself.

*Example*: Dominant discipline is Political Economy (Rule+Class, Bureaucrat chairs). Strong secondary is Moral Governance (Rule+Morality). The cleric believes the thesis is fundamentally about moral authority and resents the bureaucrat's procedural framing.

#### The Peripheral Voice

**Trigger**: A minor discipline surfaces — enough to put a member's domain on the map, but not enough to challenge the chair.

One member has a legitimate but minor claim. They ask fewer questions, but their questions cut at an angle the other two don't see. The peripheral voice can be an unexpected ally or a quiet saboteur — they have less power but more freedom.

*Example*: Dominant discipline is Natural Philosophy (Truth+Nature, Professor chairs). A minor thread of Faith+Nature (Natural Theology) gives the cleric a foothold. The cleric's questions are infrequent but theological, probing whether the Writer's empiricism has room for the divine.

#### The Absent Authority

**Trigger**: The dominant discipline produces a field so obscure or cross-disciplinary that no committee member is truly qualified to evaluate it.

A secret scenario. The committee has a gap — all three members know they're judging work that doesn't sit naturally in any of their domains. They compensate by being either overly generous (nobody wants to expose their ignorance) or overly hostile (nobody wants to approve work they don't understand). The defence becomes a performance of authority rather than an exercise of it.

*Example*: A thesis dominated by Aesthetic Naturalism (Art+Nature). The professor has some claim, but the thesis is more about beauty than method. The cleric has no foothold. The bureaucrat has no policy angle. All three are bluffing.

#### The Gatekeeper

**Trigger**: One committee member's domain is entirely absent from the thesis.

A member with no disciplinary stake in the work shifts to judging **method, form, and procedure** rather than content. They don't care what the Writer argues — they care how. Are the citations proper? Is the structure sound? Does the thesis follow approved academic conventions? The Gatekeeper is the most dangerous member because they can fail a thesis on form alone, regardless of substance.

*Example*: A thesis in Moral Theology (Faith+Morality). The bureaucrat has no domain stake. They stop asking about ethics and start asking about formatting, precedent, and institutional compliance. The Writer must defend the work's form as well as its content.

## Discipline and the Ending

At the ending — after the defence, after the committee's judgment — the Writer is alone. The private name appears for the first and only time, in the Writer's internal narration. It is what the Writer calls their work when no one is listening.

The private name is not a reward or a title. It is a recognition — sometimes proud, sometimes bitter, sometimes bewildered — of what the work became. The Writer chose to examine certain objects, develop certain ideas, write certain arguments. The discipline was never assigned. It emerged. The private name is the Writer's acknowledgment of that emergence.

This moment should feel like the Writer seeing the shape of their own thought for the first time.

## Design Notes

- The discipline is determined by the **written work**, not by domain levels. A player who develops many Nature ideas but writes mostly about Rule and Faith has a Political Theology thesis, not a Nature thesis. Writing is a deliberate act; the discipline reflects deliberate choices.
- The committee spins are attitudes, not scripts. They should inform dialogue tone and question framing without being read verbatim.
- The Absent Authority should be rare — most discipline pairs give at least one member a strong claim. Reserve it for genuinely orphaned fields (Aesthetic Naturalism, Cultural Economy, Agricultural Philosophy).
- The private names should be authored carefully. They are the last thing the player reads about the work. They should resonate with the specific tension of that discipline — not just describe it, but *feel* it.
- A thesis with four or more contributing domains surfaces six or more disciplines. This is ambitious play — the committee dynamics will be complex, and the defence harder. This is by design: groundbreaking work invites more scrutiny.
