# Recipes

How ideas are produced from other ideas. Two kinds of recipe:

1. **Development** — dwelling on a single idea advances it one level. The result is a new, higher-level idea.
2. **Combination** — two ideas collide and produce a third idea that didn't exist before.

Recipes are authored, not algorithmic. The player cannot predict what a combination will produce from tags alone. Discovery is part of the game.

### Development and Orthodoxy

Most development preserves the orthodoxy of the source idea. But some ideas sit at inflection points where development could go either way. At the Argument-to-Thesis transition, the player makes a deliberate choice:

> "You could frame this as [orthodox interpretation] or as [heretical interpretation]."

These branching points should be flagged in the Source Idea's notes.

## Combination Recipes

Two specific ideas, combined, produce a specific third idea. The result:
- Is tagged with concepts drawn from both parents (not necessarily the union — the recipe defines the result's tags)
- Sits at a level defined by the recipe (typically the higher parent's level, but recipes can override)
- Has orthodoxy influenced by both parents
- Has authored text (or generated placeholder)

### Combination Design Principles

- **Not every pair works.** Trying an invalid combination should produce a response ("The thoughts don't connect. Not yet, perhaps.") without costing time.
- **Results should surprise.** The point of combination is the unexpected connection. If the result is obvious from the ingredients, the recipe isn't interesting enough.
- **Cross-concept collisions are the most interesting.** Rule + Art should produce something neither parent anticipated.
- **Some results should be higher-level than either parent.** Combination can be a shortcut — two Inklings producing an Idea, for instance.
- **Orthodoxy can shift in combination.** An orthodox idea combined with a heretical one might produce something heterodox, or something that reframes both.

### Failed Combinations

When two ideas don't have a recipe, the response should vary:
- If the ideas share a concept: "The thoughts are too similar. They don't create friction."
- If the ideas are too far apart in level: "One thought overwhelms the other. They can't meet as equals."
- Default: "You hold them together, but nothing catches. Not yet."

---

## Notes

- Recipes are the core creative work of the game design. Each one is a small act of philosophy — asserting that THIS thought and THAT thought produce THIS new insight.
- Aim for recipes that feel retrospectively inevitable. The player should think "of course" after seeing the result, not "why?"
- Some recipes should be discoverable only after certain objects have been examined or certain narrative beats have passed (future feature).
- Track which recipes have been playtested for balance (time cost vs. reward).
